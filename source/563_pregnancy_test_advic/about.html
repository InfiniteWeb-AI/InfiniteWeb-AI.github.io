Our Mission

Pregnancy Test Advice is designed to make decisions around pregnancy testing clearer, calmer, and more informed. Many people search for answers late at night, after a missed period, or while waiting for test results. Our goal is to provide straightforward, evidence-based information so you can understand your options and have better conversations with your own healthcare professional.

Specifically, this site aims to:

  • Explain how pregnancy tests work and what can affect accuracy.
  • Help you decide when to test, using calculators and guides.
  • Clarify what different results may mean, including faint lines, evaporation lines, and errors.
  • Summarize differences between test products without promoting any particular brand.

Our content is written for anyone who may be taking a pregnancy test: people trying to conceive, those worried about an unplanned pregnancy, and those monitoring results after fertility treatment. We try to use inclusive, plain language and explain medical terms when they are necessary.

Our Editorial Process

We follow a structured editorial process so that the information you read is clear, accurate, and appropriately referenced. This process covers how we choose topics, how we research them, and how we review content before and after publication.

We prioritize topics that are most relevant to pregnancy testing decisions, such as when to test, how to read results, and when to seek medical care. We also look at:

  • Common questions asked in our Community Q&A.
  • Areas where medical guidelines have changed or may be confusing.
  • Feedback and suggestions submitted through our contact page.

We do not accept payment from pregnancy test manufacturers to create or prioritize specific topics.

Our articles and guides are based primarily on:

  • Peer-reviewed medical research and systematic reviews.
  • Guidelines from professional bodies (for example, obstetrics, gynecology, and fertility societies).
  • Trusted public health organizations and regulatory agencies.
  • Product information from test manufacturers, where relevant.

We aim to summarize complex information without oversimplifying it. Where an important uncertainty or disagreement exists in the medical literature, we say so directly instead of presenting one answer as absolute.

Articles are written by health writers and editors with experience summarizing medical information for the public. On article pages, you will usually see fields such as “By [Author Name]” based on the author_name stored for each article.

Writers are expected to:

  • Use up-to-date, reputable sources.
  • Disclose conflicts of interest related to pregnancy testing or fertility services.
  • Write in clear, respectful language that recognizes different pregnancy goals and experiences.

Each article has a published_at date, and many also show a “Last reviewed” or “Last updated” date. We aim to review key content on a regular schedule and when:

  • New research changes what is known about pregnancy testing.
  • Guidelines from trusted medical organizations are updated.
  • Users or medical reviewers alert us to potential inaccuracies or unclear explanations.

When we make corrections or clarifications, we aim to note them clearly in the article, especially if they change the meaning of earlier advice.

Medical Review Practices

Many, but not all, articles on Pregnancy Test Advice are medically reviewed. Medical review means that a qualified clinician has examined the content for accuracy and balance based on current evidence and guidelines.

Medical reviewers are healthcare professionals such as physicians, nurse practitioners, or other clinicians with relevant training in obstetrics, gynecology, fertility, or primary care. On medically reviewed articles, you may see labels such as “Medically reviewed by [Name, Credentials]”, based on the article’s reviewer_name and medically_reviewed fields.

Reviewers are asked to disclose any financial relationships or conflicts of interest that could influence their assessment of pregnancy testing or related treatments.

During medical review, the clinician:

  • Checks whether the article reflects current evidence and major guidelines.
  • Flags statements that may be incomplete, misleading, or unclear.
  • Suggests changes to improve balance, especially where evidence is limited or evolving.
  • Highlights when readers should be explicitly encouraged to see a healthcare professional.

Editors then revise the article in response to this feedback. In some cases, multiple rounds of review are needed before publication.

On article or guide pages, medically reviewed content is usually marked by:

  • A badge or label such as “Medically reviewed”.
  • The reviewer’s name and credentials, when available.
  • A “Last reviewed” date near the top of the article.

Articles that have not yet been medically reviewed may still be accurate and carefully sourced, but they have not been checked by a clinician. The presence or absence of a medical review label does not replace your own doctor’s advice about your specific situation.

Disclaimer

The information on Pregnancy Test Advice is for general educational purposes only. It is not a substitute for professional medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment.

Our tools, articles, and product information are designed to help you understand pregnancy testing, not to tell you what you should personally do. They cannot:

  • Provide a medical diagnosis.
  • Replace an in-person or telehealth consultation.
  • Take into account all of your health history, medications, or other conditions.

Never delay or avoid seeking professional medical advice because of something you read here.

You should seek advice from a qualified healthcare professional whenever:

  • You are unsure how to interpret a pregnancy test result.
  • Your period is significantly late or irregular for reasons you do not understand.
  • You have ongoing pelvic pain, heavy bleeding, or other concerning symptoms.
  • You have a medical condition or use medications that may affect pregnancy or testing.

Local healthcare services, telehealth providers, or family planning clinics can help you interpret results and discuss next steps.

If you think you might be experiencing a medical emergency, do not use this website for advice.

Instead, seek immediate care by:

  • Calling your local emergency number right away (for example, 911 in the United States), or
  • Going directly to the nearest emergency department or urgent care clinic.

Possible emergency warning signs can include, but are not limited to:

  • Severe abdominal or pelvic pain.
  • Heavy vaginal bleeding (for example, soaking through pads every hour).
  • Feeling faint, dizzy, or short of breath.
  • Chest pain or sudden, severe headache.

This list is not complete. Trust your instincts—if you are worried that something is seriously wrong, seek urgent care.

Our tools (such as the When to Test Calculator, Symptom Checker, and Planner) are based on typical cycle lengths, product information, and published research. However:

  • Results are estimates and may not be accurate for every person or situation.
  • They cannot predict pregnancy or guarantee a particular test result.
  • They may not account for all medical conditions, medications, or fertility treatments.

Community Q&A content represents the views and experiences of individual users, not Pregnancy Test Advice as a whole. Expert answers, where offered, are for general information only and do not create a doctor–patient relationship.

  • A brief description of the issue.
  • Any supporting references (for example, guideline links or research articles).
